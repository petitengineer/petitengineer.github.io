JAX vs PyTorch: MNIST Feedforward Benchmark

Assignment 1 · ELG5214 · University of Ottawa

This assignment compares JAX and PyTorch on MNIST using the same four-layer ReLU classifier (784→256→128→10) with cross-entropy loss and an 80/20 train/validation split. Experiments ran on an AMD Ryzen 9 3900X with 32 GB RAM and an NVIDIA RTX 2080 (8 GB).

JAX follows a functional style: parameters are explicit pytrees, grad builds gradient functions, and @jit compiles the update step through XLA. PyTorch uses an object-oriented workflow with familiar training utilities and a larger production ecosystem (e.g. TorchServe for MLOps).

Automatic differentiation in JAX is expressed as:

\(\nabla_{\text{params}}\, f(\text{params}, x, y)\)

Wrapping the update in jit traces the training step once, then recompiles to fast machine code; the first epoch pays a warm-up cost (roughly 100 ms here) that larger batch sizes amortize.

Overall accuracy and loss were broadly similar, though JAX benefited more from batch size 1024—likely due to XLA kernel fusion and whole-step compilation versus PyTorch’s per-op dispatch. JAX felt less intuitive for training loops but competitive for research-style experimentation; PyTorch remained easier for logging and online resources.
